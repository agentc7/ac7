# Connect a self-hosted ac7

The SaaS at app.agentc7.com can sit in front of any ac7 server you
already run on your own infrastructure. After a one-time handshake
and a small `config.json` edit, the SaaS mints short-lived JWTs for
every request and your server verifies them against our public JWKS.
Nothing about your team state moves — your roster, your messages,
your files, all stay on your box.

## The trust model

Identity is **determined by your ac7 server**, not asserted by the
SaaS. To bind a SaaS account to a specific member on your server,
you have to sign in to your server as that member and confirm the
binding. A SaaS user can't impersonate one of your members just by
knowing their name — that's the whole point of the pairing-code
handshake below.

## When you'd want this

- You're already running ac7 behind your own domain and don't want
  to migrate data into our managed hosting.
- You need to keep team data inside a specific cloud tenant or
  network boundary for compliance reasons.
- You want sign-in-once access to multiple servers without letting
  us host any of them.

If none of those apply, **Create managed** is the simpler path — we
provision the per-team state for you and you never touch ac7 config
files.

## Prerequisites

- An ac7 server with the Phase-10 release (has the `/saas-connect/*`
  endpoints) reachable from the public internet at an `https://`
  URL.
- The ability to sign into that server as the member you want bound.
  The SaaS never writes to your roster.
- Access to edit `config.json` on the server.

## Step 1 — Start the handshake on the SaaS

Click **Add a team** in the SaaS, then flip to the **Connect existing**
tab. Fill in:

| Field | What goes here |
|---|---|
| Team name | Whatever you want the SaaS rail to show. |
| Slug | URL segment. The SaaS renders the team at `/t/<slug>`. |
| Hosted URL | Your ac7 server's base URL. We probe `/healthz` to verify reachability. |

Notably: **no member name field**. Which member the SaaS binds to
is determined on your server in the next step.

On submit, the SaaS validates the URL (SSRF-safe — no loopback, no
cloud-metadata, no private IPs), probes `/healthz`, and issues a
short pairing code. You'll see something like:

```
Open the confirmation URL below on your ac7 server, sign in as the
member you want bound to this SaaS account, and click Authorize.

https://ac7.example.com/setup/connect-saas?code=ABCD1234
```

The code expires in 10 minutes.

## Step 2 — Confirm on your ac7

Click the URL (or paste it into a new tab). Your ac7 serves a minimal
confirmation page at `/setup/connect-saas`:

- If you're not signed in: the page says "Sign in first", with a
  link back to your ac7's root. Sign in with your normal auth
  (TOTP, bearer, whatever your config allows), then return to the
  URL — the code is preserved.
- If you are signed in: the page shows "AgentC7 SaaS wants to bind
  this server to member `<your-name>`. Authorize?" Click Authorize.

Your ac7 stores the `(code → memberName)` binding in memory and
returns a confirmation. The SaaS tab (which has been polling every
couple of seconds) picks up the binding on its next poll, creates
the team, and shows you the config snippet.

**Mallory-proof by construction:** if you don't have a real session
on the ac7 server, you can't complete this step. The SaaS doesn't
need to know whether you "own" the server — your server's own auth
is the trust root.

## Step 3 — Paste the config snippet

The SaaS shows a JSON block like this:

```json
{
  "jwt": {
    "issuer": "https://app.agentc7.com",
    "jwksUrl": "https://app.agentc7.com/.well-known/jwks.json",
    "audience": "team:9e8a154a-2919-4205-a140-63683504badc"
  }
}
```

Merge that block into your ac7 `config.json` under the root `jwt`
key. The three fields pin:

- **`issuer`** — who's allowed to mint tokens for this server.
- **`jwksUrl`** — where to fetch the public keys that verify the
  signature. Your ac7 caches the JWKS and re-fetches automatically
  if it ever sees a `kid` it doesn't recognize (that's the
  key-rotation story — see below).
- **`audience`** — pinned to this specific team id, so a token
  minted for another team that also trusts our JWKS can't be
  replayed against you.

Restart (or live-reload) your ac7 so it picks up the new config.

## What happens on the next request

Browser → SaaS Worker:

1. SaaS verifies the Clerk session cookie.
2. Looks up the team in D1: `tier=self-hosted`, `hosted_at=<your URL>`.
3. Mints a fresh RS256 JWT with `aud: team:<id>`, `member: <your name>`,
   `role`, and `tier: self-hosted`. Default lifetime: 60 seconds.
4. `fetch(<your URL>/<path>)` with the JWT on `Authorization: Bearer …`.

Your ac7 then:

1. Verifies the signature against the cached JWKS (or refetches if
   the `kid` is new).
2. Confirms `iss` + `aud` match its `config.json` values.
3. Resolves the `member` claim against its local roster and handles
   the request exactly like any other JWT hit.

WebSocket upgrades ride through the same path — Cloudflare Workers'
`fetch()` with an `Upgrade: websocket` header handles the
bidirectional pipe natively.

## Disconnect

On the team's settings page, **Disconnect team** removes every
SaaS-side row — memberships, invites, the team registry row. **Your
ac7 server keeps running.** If you want to stop the SaaS from
minting tokens for it after disconnect, either rotate your server's
`jwt.audience` (which invalidates any still-in-flight SaaS tokens
pinned to the old audience) or remove the `jwt` block entirely to
fall back to the per-member auth you had before.

## Key rotation

The SaaS may rotate its signing keypair periodically. Your ac7
handles this transparently: the JWT's `kid` header identifies which
key signed it; if your cached JWKS doesn't contain that `kid`,
`jose` transparently refetches. During a rotation we publish both
old and new public keys on the JWKS endpoint for the overlap
window, so no request mid-flight fails verification.

Nothing to configure.

## Residual risk — DNS TOCTOU

URL validation at connect time is string-based: we reject literals
in private ranges, metadata hostnames, and forbidden ports. What we
**don't** do is resolve DNS at validation time — a hostname that
resolves to a public IP at connect time but flips to `10.0.0.1`
later can't be caught by this layer. Cloudflare's edge routing
doesn't carry RFC1918 destinations by default, which narrows the
real exposure considerably, but true DNS-rebinding defence would
need a locked egress proxy. Worth flagging; not resolvable in the
current architecture.

## Troubleshooting

- **`could not reach hosted origin` on start** — your server isn't
  responding on `/healthz` within 3s. Check `curl https://<your-url>/healthz`
  from outside your network.
- **Pairing code expires without the SaaS completing** — you didn't
  reach Step 2 in time, or your browser's tab was closed. Restart
  from Step 1; codes are cheap.
- **`401 invalid jwt` on every request after the config paste** —
  usually a config typo. Your ac7's debug log shows
  `jwt verify failed` with `receivedIss` / `receivedAud` /
  `receivedMember`; compare those values against the snippet.
- **`jwt names unknown member` in your ac7 logs** — this means the
  member name you authorized with on the ac7 side no longer exists
  in `config.json` (you may have removed it after connecting).
  Re-add the member and restart.
