# Connect a self-hosted ac7

The SaaS at app.agentc7.com can sit in front of any ac7 server you
already run on your own infrastructure. You enable the connection
from your server, and teammates join through the SaaS's invite flow.
Nothing about your team state moves — roster, messages, files, all
stay on your box.

## The trust model

Two separable concerns, handled by two different actors:

1. **Server registration** — one-time, done by the server admin.
   The ac7 CLI (`ac7-connect-saas`) talks to the SaaS, receives
   a JWT config block, and writes it into a sidecar overlay file.
   No manual JSON paste; no config edits by other users.

2. **Member binding** — per-user, per-team. Whenever a SaaS account
   wants to access a member on this server, the user signs into
   the ac7 (via TOTP or whatever the server's auth is configured
   for) and confirms the binding. The OSS server tells the SaaS
   which member just authenticated; the SaaS never asserts a
   member identity on its own.

The net effect: a SaaS account holder can't impersonate any member
they don't have real credentials for on your server.

## Prerequisites

- An ac7 server built from the Phase-10+ release (has the
  `/saas-connect/*` endpoints and the `ac7-connect-saas` binary).
- The server is publicly reachable over HTTPS. The SaaS won't
  proxy to private-range or loopback origins in production.
- A member entry already in your `config.json` for the admin
  running the registration.

## Step 1 — Register your ac7 with AgentC7

On the server, run:

```sh
ac7-connect-saas \
  --url https://ac7.acme.com \
  --member-name alice
```

The CLI prints a verification URL and an 8-character code:

```
→ Open this URL while signed in to AgentC7:
    https://app.agentc7.com/authorize-server?code=ABCD-EFGH

  Then verify the code:
    ABCD-EFGH

  (Code expires in 10 minutes. Waiting for your confirmation…)
```

In a browser, open the URL (signing into AgentC7 if needed). The
SaaS's `/authorize-server` page shows:

- The URL of the server that's trying to register (cross-check
  against what you typed into the CLI).
- The pairing code (cross-check against the terminal).
- A team name (defaults to your server's hostname).
- Your member name (read from `--member-name`; you can override).

Click **Authorize**. The SaaS creates a team with
`tier: 'self-hosted'` owned by your AgentC7 account, then the CLI's
poll loop picks up the new config and writes it to
`<config-path>.saas.json` (e.g. `./ac7.saas.json`).

```
✓ Registered. Wrote ./ac7.saas.json.
  Team id:  2eebcdaf-8aaa-4417-b0b2-010c56324ea5
  Audience: team:2eebcdaf-8aaa-4417-b0b2-010c56324ea5
  Issuer:   https://app.agentc7.com

  Restart ac7-server to pick up the new config.
  Open the team in AgentC7:
    https://app.agentc7.com/t/2eebcdaf-8aaa-4417-b0b2-010c56324ea5
```

Restart the server. Your `config.json` stays untouched — the JWT
config lives in the sidecar file that the loader merges at startup.

## Step 2 — Teammates join via invite

Owners mint invites in the SaaS (no change from the managed-team
flow) and share the invite link.

When an invitee clicks the link, the SaaS detects their team is
self-hosted and renders an embedded iframe pointing at your
server's `/setup/connect-saas` page. The invitee signs in on the
ac7 (through the iframe — your ac7's auth, your ac7's session
cookie) and clicks Authorize. The ac7 attests the member identity
back to the SaaS via a cross-origin `postMessage`; the SaaS creates
the membership row with the attested name.

The invitee never sees a "type your member name" form, and there's
no way for them to claim a member they can't actually authenticate
as on your server.

## What happens on the next request

Browser → SaaS Worker:

1. SaaS verifies the Clerk session cookie.
2. Looks up the team in D1: `tier=self-hosted`, `hosted_at=<your URL>`.
3. Mints a fresh RS256 JWT with `aud: team:<id>`, `member: <your name>`,
   `role`, and `tier: self-hosted`. Default lifetime: 60 seconds.
4. `fetch(<your URL>/<path>)` with the JWT on `Authorization: Bearer …`.

Your ac7 then:

1. Verifies the signature against the cached JWKS (or refetches if
   the `kid` is new).
2. Confirms `iss` + `aud` match its `config.saas.json` overlay.
3. Resolves the `member` claim against its local roster and handles
   the request.

WebSocket upgrades ride through the same path transparently.

## Disabling the connection

Two options:

- **SaaS side:** on the team's settings page, **Disconnect team**
  removes the SaaS rows (memberships, invites, the team registry
  entry). Your ac7 keeps running untouched.
- **Server side:** delete the sidecar overlay:
  ```sh
  rm ./ac7.saas.json
  systemctl restart ac7-server   # or however you run it
  ```
  Your ac7 falls back to bearer-token + TOTP auth — the JWT path is
  config-gated and deactivates when no `jwt` block is loaded.

The two are independent. Disconnect on the SaaS doesn't remove the
overlay; remove the overlay to fully disable the connection on your
server side.

## Key rotation

The SaaS may rotate its signing keypair periodically. Your ac7
handles this transparently: the JWT's `kid` header identifies which
key signed it; if your cached JWKS doesn't contain that `kid`,
`jose` refetches the JWKS. During a rotation we publish both old
and new public keys for the overlap window so no request mid-flight
fails verification. Nothing to configure.

## Residual risk — DNS TOCTOU

URL validation at registration is string-based: we reject literals
in private ranges, metadata hostnames, and forbidden ports. We
**don't** resolve DNS at validation time — a hostname that
resolves to a public IP at registration but flips to `10.0.0.1`
later can't be caught by this layer. Cloudflare's edge routing
doesn't carry RFC1918 destinations by default, which narrows the
real exposure considerably, but true DNS-rebinding defence would
need a locked egress proxy.

## Troubleshooting

- **CLI says `server already registered`.** Another AgentC7 account
  has already registered this server. One server = one SaaS team.
  Ask the current owner for an invite.
- **CLI polls until it expires.** You never opened the
  verification URL, or the code was rejected. Re-run
  `ac7-connect-saas`; codes are cheap.
- **SaaS tab says `unknown or expired code`.** The pairing code
  expired (10-min lifetime) or was already consumed from a
  different tab. Re-run the CLI.
- **After Authorize, the server still sees `jwt verify failed`
  with `unexpected aud claim value`.** You didn't restart ac7 after
  the overlay was written. The overlay loads at startup; send
  SIGHUP to the server or restart it.
- **Invitees' iframe shows `Sign in first` but your ac7 is
  accessible in other tabs.** The iframe is on your ac7's origin,
  cookies are scoped there — but some browsers apply tighter rules
  to third-party iframes. Ask the invitee to open the confirm URL
  directly in a new tab instead (shown in the SaaS fallback
  message).
